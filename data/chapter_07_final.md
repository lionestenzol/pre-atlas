# THE VALIDATION TRAP

### Power Dynamics Mastery Guide - Chapter 7

---

The most dangerous game you can play is the one where you need someone else to tell you who you are. And almost everybody plays it without realizing they've started.

I played it for years. Not obviously -- I wasn't walking around asking people if they liked me. It was subtler than that. I'd do something well and then check to see if people noticed. I'd set a boundary and then look for confirmation that I'd done the right thing. I'd make a decision and then wait to see if someone validated it before I felt confident about it.

That's the validation trap. It looks like confidence from the outside, but underneath, every action is contingent on someone else's approval. And the moment that approval gets delayed or denied, the whole structure wobbles.

---

## The Seeking Pattern

Seeking validation is a loop, not a line. You don't do it once and stop. You do it once, get the hit of approval, feel good for a moment, and then need it again. And again. And again. Each time, the satisfaction lasts a little shorter. Each time, you need a little more.

I noticed this at work. I'd deliver something excellent and wait for the manager to acknowledge it. If they did, I felt great. If they didn't, I spiraled. Not externally -- internally. I'd start questioning whether the work was actually good, whether I was in the right place, whether I was valued. All because one person didn't say the words I was waiting for.

That's an insane amount of power to hand someone. I was giving another person the ability to determine my emotional state based on whether they chose to give me three seconds of acknowledgment. And most of the time, they weren't even thinking about it. They had their own problems. My work was fine. The silence wasn't a judgment -- it was just silence. But I'd turned it into a verdict.

---

## How Testing Becomes Seeking

Here's where it gets complicated. Some people aren't testing you -- they're looking to you for guidance. They don't know what to do when they're around someone that's strong, so they're looking for someone in power to show them the way.

I spent a long time misreading that signal. I thought everyone who challenged me was hostile. But some of them were doing the same thing I was doing -- seeking validation. They pushed back not because they wanted to defeat me, but because they wanted to see if I was real. If I was as solid as I appeared. They were testing my authenticity, not my strength.

The problem is that testing and seeking look identical on the surface. Both involve pushback, friction, and discomfort. The difference is in what happens after. If someone tests you and you hold your ground and they respect that, they were seeking. If someone tests you and you hold your ground and they escalate, they were competing.

Learning to tell the difference saved me from burning bridges with people who were actually trying to connect.

---

## The Insult to Self

The deepest cut of the validation trap isn't when other people won't validate you. It's when you won't validate yourself.

Someone once told me I was too much. Too intense. Too driven. Too focused. And instead of recognizing that as their limitation, I absorbed it as my flaw. I spent weeks trying to be less. Less intense. Less driven. Less myself. And you know what happened? I felt terrible. Because dimming yourself to avoid someone else's discomfort is the ultimate self-betrayal.

The validation I needed wasn't from them. It was from me. I needed to look at who I was -- all the intensity, all the drive, all the parts that made people uncomfortable -- and say: this is who I am, and it's not wrong just because someone else can't handle it.

That shift -- from seeking external validation to generating internal validation -- is the single most important thing I've ever done for my mental health. Because external validation is unreliable. People give it when it's convenient and withhold it when it's not. But internal validation is always available. It doesn't depend on someone else's mood or agenda.

---

## Ownership as the Exit

The way out of the validation trap is ownership. Complete ownership of who you are, what you want, and what you will and won't tolerate.

I know I look good. I know my work is solid. I know my boundaries are reasonable. I know my drive is a strength. I don't need anyone to confirm that for me anymore. And the moment I stopped needing confirmation, the confirmation started flowing in from everywhere. Because people can sense when you're secure. And security is magnetic.

That's the paradox. When you stop needing validation, you get more of it than ever. Because the energy you used to spend seeking -- the looking, the waiting, the adjusting -- gets replaced by an energy that's purely focused on doing the work. And people are drawn to that focus. They respect it. They validate it voluntarily.

But here's the key: by the time they do, you don't need it anymore. And that's how you know you're free.

---

## The Idea Trap

The validation trap doesn't just apply to personal interactions. It applies to your ideas, your ambitions, your plans.

I had ideas that I held back for years because nobody around me could see what I saw. I'd pitch something and get blank stares. I'd describe a vision and get "that's interesting" in the tone that really means "I have no idea what you're talking about." And each time, I'd file the idea away, thinking: maybe it's not as good as I thought.

But the ideas weren't the problem. The audience was the problem. I was seeking validation from people who didn't have the context, the vision, or the capacity to understand what I was building. That's like asking a fish to validate your mountain climbing plans.

The moment I stopped needing other people to cosign my vision, I started building it. And building is the best validation there is. Because when the thing exists -- when it works, when it produces results -- the people who doubted it don't get to pretend they saw it coming. The work speaks for itself.

---

## The Respect vs. Validation Distinction

Respect and validation look similar, but they're completely different currencies. Respect is earned through consistent behavior. Validation is sought through emotional need.

I respect people who show up, do the work, and hold their standards. That's earned. But I don't need them to validate me in return. I don't need them to tell me I'm doing a good job or that they're impressed. If they do, great. If they don't, nothing changes.

When you operate from respect instead of validation, your relationships transform. You stop keeping score. You stop tracking who noticed and who didn't. You stop performing for an audience and start building for yourself. And the people around you feel the difference. They feel that you're not taking from the interaction. You're just being present in it. And that presence -- that lack of need -- is the most attractive quality a person can have.

---

## The Laws

**Validation from others is rented, never owned.** It can be given and withdrawn at any time for any reason. The only validation you truly own is the kind you give yourself.

**Seeking validation is a loop, not a destination.** Each hit of approval wears off faster. The cycle only breaks when you stop needing the next hit.

**Not everyone who challenges you is hostile.** Some people push back because they're seeking proof that you're real. Learn to tell seekers from competitors. It'll save you from burning good bridges.

**Shrinking to satisfy others is self-betrayal.** If someone says you're "too much," that's their capacity problem, not your personality flaw. Never dim yourself to make someone else's darkness comfortable.

**Build before you pitch.** Don't wait for permission or agreement to pursue your vision. Build first. The work is its own validation. Results silence doubt faster than any argument.

**Security is magnetic.** When you stop needing people to tell you who you are, they start wanting to. Internal certainty attracts external respect. That's the paradox and the reward.

---

The validation trap is the quietest cage there is. You can't see the bars because they're made of other people's opinions. But the door was never locked. You just have to decide that your own assessment of yourself matters more than anyone else's. Once you make that decision -- really make it, not just say it -- the trap dissolves. And you walk through the rest of your life with the one thing that nobody can take from you: certainty about who you are.
