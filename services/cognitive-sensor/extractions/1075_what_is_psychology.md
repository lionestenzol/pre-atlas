## Extracted Frameworks

### Outsider Navigation Protocol
When entering an unfamiliar social hierarchy as a complete outsider:
- Initial novelty/curiosity phase (people don't know how to categorize you)
- Observation period (learning the unwritten rules before participating)
- Selective adaptation (adopting enough of the local code to communicate, without losing core identity)
- Competence-based ascent (earning respect through demonstrated ability rather than inherited status)

### Dual-Hierarchy Dynamics
In environments where two status systems coexist (suburban/money vs. urban/authenticity):
- Each group has leverage the other wants
- Suburban mimics urban for cultural credibility; urban resents the imitation
- The person who can navigate both systems without fully belonging to either has a unique vantage point
- Verbal conflict (roasting, jonesing) serves as the primary status arbitration mechanism

### Authority-Ability Gap
Recurring pattern across contexts (school, workplace, relationships):
- Having high ability but low formal authority creates envy and sabotage from those with authority but lower ability
- The gap itself is a weapon — opponents underestimate you because your title doesn't match your capability
- Strategy: let opponents overextend while you accumulate evidence and allies, then act decisively

### Opponent-as-Asset Pattern
People who conspire against you often end up doing your work:
- The coworker who tried to steal ideas became the main promoter of the slogan
- The coworker who blocked the team leader still helped hit the highest numbers
- People who "do you dirty end up working on your behalf"
- Key insight: sometimes not confronting someone IS the strategy, because they're more useful as an unwitting asset

### Emotional Investment as Fungible Resource
- Attachment isn't progressive — it's "Russian Roulette" (all-or-nothing bets)
- Once a person's unique value is replaced (better sex, more attention, new environment), the attachment dissolves cleanly
- This applies across romantic, platonic, and professional relationships
- Coworkers, friends, exes all follow the same depreciation curve once their function is served
